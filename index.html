Part 1. How does the hydrocarbon get trapped in the Soil and what happens thereon?

Step 1: Phytoplantons die and hit the ocean floor to decompose.

Oil comes from the remains of tiny plants and animals (plankton) that died in ancient seas between 10 million and 600 million years ago.After the organisms died, they sank into the sand and mud at the bottom of the sea. Usually they are eaten by other aquatic organisms and never hit the ocean floor.

Step 2: Layers of mud and soil accumulate decomposing and trapping the dead Phytoplantons

Over the years, the organisms decayed in the sedimentary layers. In these layers, there is little to no oxygen present. So microorganisms broke the remains into carbon-rich compounds that form organic layers. The organic material mixes with the sediments, forming fine-grained shale, or source rock. As more sedimentary layers are deposited, they exert intense pressure and heat on the source rock. The heat and pressure distills the organic material into crude oil and natural gas.


Step 3: Oil and natural gas rise up to reservoir rock.

The oil flows from the source rock and accumulats in thicker, more porous limestone or sandstone, called reservoir rock. Movements in the Earth traps the oil and natural gas in the reservoir rocks between layers of impermeable rock, or cap rock, such as granite or marble.These movements of the Earth include: Folding, Faulting, Pinching out



Step 4: Oil is found and extracted

Whether employed directly by an oil company or under contract from a private firm, geologists are the ones responsible for finding oil. Geologists use sensitive gravity meters to measure tiny changes in the Earth's gravitational field that could indicate flowing oil, as well as sensitive magnetometers to measure tiny changes in the Earth's magnetic field caused by flowing oil. They also use seismology, creating shock waves that pass through hidden rock layers and interpreting the waves that are reflected back to the surface. Once located, oil companies send out rigs to drill holes into the ocean floor to extract oil.




Step 5: What is in the water now?

During drilling and post drilling, chemicals and toxic substances are released.In addition, if sesimec waves are used to locate oil, they can diorient sea animals like whales stranding them on beach.And, oil spills can damage marine life and environment to a large extent.
